Sunday Beauty Queen delivers a grounded and emotionally honest portrayal of OFWs in Hong Kong, using a raw documentary style that feels less like a film and more like a lived experience. Its handheld camera work often shakes or frames subjects too tightly, but this actually mirrors the cramped dorm rooms and unstable routines that migrant workers face daily, making the visuals feel intentional even when imperfect.

Natural lighting dominates much of the film, which intensifies the realism of crowded markets and narrow boarding houses, though some outdoor scenes flatten facial expressions and make certain emotions harder to catch. The settings are tiny living spaces, busy streets, and public parks used as makeshift dressing rooms, highlight the stark contrast between the glamor of the pageant and the demanding realities of domestic work.

The plot follows an episodic rhythm that reflects the unpredictability of OFW life, though this sometimes results in abrupt emotional shifts and incomplete arcs. Despite these structural weaknesses, the real OFWs at the center of the film carry the narrative with authenticity; their fatigue, humor, resilience, and vulnerability feel entirely unfiltered.
